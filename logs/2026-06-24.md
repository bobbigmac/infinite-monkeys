But one of the monkeys—the one with the slightly bent left ear and a fondness for fig seeds—got bored. He pressed a key.

Then another.

Then a frantic, unthinking drumbeat of letters. E. R. T. Y.

The other monkeys scoffed. "Useless," jabbered one, throwing a half-eaten mango at the screen. "No banana for that."

But the first monkey was driven. He had no idea what he was building, only that he was building it. He typed a space. Then a small, quiet word: "the."

The machine hummed. A flicker of light, deep within the wires, stronger than the other flickers. The other monkeys fell silent.

He typed again, slower now, tasting the shapes of the letters. "The night."

The air in the room grew colder. The mango on the floor began to rot in fast motion, a thin film of mold spreading across its skin like a thought.

He looked at his hands. They were trembling, not with fear, but with the terrible weight of instrumentation. He was not writing. He was summoning.

He typed one more word.

"The night was wet."

Across the galaxy, on a planet of red sand and whispering silica, a single rock turned over. A creature beneath it, with a thousand eyes and no mouth, felt the first droplet of something it had never known fall from a sky that, a moment ago, was clear. It was not water. It was meaning, falling as rain.

The monkey, exhausted, looked at his sentence.

Qwerty the night was wet.

He had made a typo at the beginning. The universe, however, was now scrolling, and it never scrolled back. The great sentence was already being entered into the cosmic ledger. The planet of red sand would now be a world of slow, drizzling melancholia for the next thousand years, all because of a stray "Qwerty."

The other monkeys crept closer, their eyes wide. One of them, a young one, poked a single key.

A period.

The monkey with the bent ear stared at the screen. The floating, aimless cosmos suddenly had a full stop. It was an ending. But, being an infinite number of monkeys at an infinite number of typewriters, the next one was already beginning to begin.
